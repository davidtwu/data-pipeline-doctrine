## Post Ideas


- Write a blog post on the Non-Obvious Problem Reframe, and how I applied it to the current project. This is the first and arguably most important PE-bar signal. Before the kickoff, you should have developed a hypothesis for why the obvious approach to this problem is wrong or insufficient. This reframe should change the solution space — not just the solution. The classic pattern is a category shift: "this isn't a data quality problem, it's a feedback loop problem" or "this isn't a latency problem, it's a caching invalidation problem." What you do: Present your reframe to the team as a hypothesis, not a decree. Explain the reasoning: what observation led you to this reframe, what evidence supports it, and what would disprove it. The reframe should pass two tests: (1) a strong L6 would not have arrived at it independently, and (2) it meaningfully changes what you'd build. What the team does: Stress-test the reframe with real data and examples from their domains. Push back hard. The insight must survive scrutiny — if it doesn't, either refine it or abandon it honestly. A PE who clings to a reframe that the evidence doesn't support is worse than one who never proposed one. What success looks like: The team's approach is meaningfully different from what would have emerged without the reframe. Stakeholders say "I hadn't thought about it that way." The reframe creates a clear before/after in how the team understands the problem. How to develop the reframe: Spend 2–3 hours before the kickoff examining the problem from first principles. Ask: what assumptions is everyone making? Where is the system feedback loop that nobody's talking about? What adjacent domain has already solved a structurally similar problem? What would you do differently if you had 10x the data, or 1/10th the latency budget? The reframe often comes from questioning a constraint that everyone else treats as fixed. Discuss how to use AI to do this.
