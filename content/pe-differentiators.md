I'm leading a new project with a small team of engineers, and I've been thinking about how to maximize my impact as a principal engineer. Not just "run the project well" — that's table stakes. But what does PE-level contribution actually look like?

After reflecting on past projects and what made some more impactful than others, I landed on eight behaviors that separate "good execution" from "PE-level leverage." The baseline work — clean execution, clear communication, stakeholder management — keeps projects from failing. These eight behaviors are what create compounding value beyond the immediate work.

---

## 1. Reframe the Problem

Develop a hypothesis for why the obvious approach is wrong or insufficient. The reframe should change the solution space, not just the solution.

Problem framing is the highest-leverage moment in any project. A reframe early costs hours; discovering the wrong framing late costs the entire project. A category shift like "this is a feedback loop problem, not a data quality problem" can reveal a 10x simpler approach.

---

## 2. Connect Work to Strategic Context

Articulate not just why the problem matters, but why solving it now creates disproportionate value.

Projects without clear strategic rationale are the first to get cut when priorities shift. Work that's visibly connected to strategic priorities gets staffing, executive attention, and cross-team cooperation.

---

## 3. Make Bold Technical Bets

Make non-consensus technical decisions where a bolder choice creates disproportionate value compared to the safe default.

Committee-driven architecture converges on mediocrity. The "safe" choice often carries hidden costs that compound over years. At the PE level, your technical judgment is your primary contribution. When you're wrong, own it and course-correct publicly — that's more valuable than always being right.

---

## 4. Permanently Raise the Bar for Engineers

Engineers who work with you should come out permanently better — carrying new skills and mental models forward indefinitely.

Code gets deprecated. Systems get replaced. But an engineer you've leveled up continues generating value for decades. If you've taught five engineers how you think about system design, your judgment is present in five rooms simultaneously.

---

## 5. Influence Beyond Your Immediate Team

The same effort that serves your project's goals should also raise the bar for the broader organization.

If you solve a problem and keep the solution to yourself, you've created value once. If you package and share it, you've created value N times. Share emerging insights with adjacent teams while work is in flight — don't wait until you're done.

---

## 6. Build Mechanisms That Outlast the Project

Leave behind something that continues generating value after you've moved on — a framework, pattern, tool, or process that others can use independently.

A project delivers value once, when it ships. A mechanism delivers value every time someone uses it. The key criteria: it must be usable by another team without your involvement.

---

## 7. Construct a Strategic Narrative

Your work should end with "here's what this makes possible," not just "here's what we built."

The narrative you construct determines how leadership thinks about follow-on investment. "We shipped a feature" invites no particular next step. "We built the foundation for X, Y, and Z" creates a roadmap.

---

## 8. Exercise Judgment Under Uncertainty

Make decisions with incomplete information, document your reasoning, and course-correct transparently when you're wrong.

In fast-moving environments, the cost of delayed decisions often exceeds the cost of slightly wrong decisions. State your confidence level, set checkpoints to validate, and pivot publicly when the evidence says you were wrong.

---

## The One-Liner

The baseline keeps projects from failing. These eight behaviors are how you create compounding value beyond the immediate work.
